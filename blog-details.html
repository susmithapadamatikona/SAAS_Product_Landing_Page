Why grounded retrieval beats a bigger model every time

Home/ Blog/ Grounded retrieval
EMElena Moreau · March 4, 2026 · 8 min read AI

Every few months a new model lands with a larger context window, and every few months a wave of teams conclude that their AI problems are about to solve themselves. They paste in more documents, the answers get marginally better, the bill gets substantially worse, and the fundamental problem stays exactly where it was.

The fundamental problem is that the model does not know which of your 40,000 documents matters for this question. Making the window bigger does not teach it. It just means it has more to be confused by.

The teams getting real value from AI are not the ones with the largest context window. They are the ones who solved retrieval.

What we tried first, and why it failed

Our first architecture was embarrassingly naive: embed everything, dump the top 20 chunks into the prompt, hope for the best. It demoed beautifully. In production it fell apart within a week for three reasons.

  • Chunks lost their context. A paragraph that says "revenue fell 12%" is meaningless without knowing which quarter, product and region it refers to.
  • Semantic similarity is not relevance. The chunk that sounds most like the question is frequently not the one that answers it — especially for numerical questions.
  • No recency weighting. A 2023 pricing doc and the current one are equally similar to "what do we charge?" and only one of them is correct.

The architecture we landed on

What works is boring, and it is mostly retrieval engineering rather than model work. Three layers, each cheap on its own.

1. Structured extraction at ingest

Before anything is embedded, we extract entities, dates, document type and ownership into a structured index. Half of the queries we see are answerable from that index alone, with no model call at all — and those answers are exactly correct rather than probably correct.

2. Hybrid retrieval with a reranker

We run keyword (BM25) and vector search in parallel, merge the candidate sets, then rerank with a small cross-encoder. The reranker is the highest-leverage component in the entire pipeline: swapping it in lifted answer accuracy by 31 points, far more than any base model upgrade we have ever made.

3. Citations as a hard requirement

Every generated claim must carry a pointer back to the row or paragraph it came from. If the model cannot cite it, we do not show it. This single constraint eliminated the overwhelming majority of hallucinations — not by making the model smarter, but by making the unsupported answer structurally impossible to render.

The results

On our internal benchmark of 2,400 real customer questions, moving from naive RAG to this pipeline took exact-match accuracy from 61% to 94%, cut median latency from 3.2s to 900ms, and reduced inference cost per query by about 70% — because most queries never reach the large model at all.

The uncomfortable conclusion for anyone hoping to buy their way out of this: the model is now the least interesting part of the system. The interesting part is the plumbing, and nobody is going to sell you plumbing that already fits your house.
